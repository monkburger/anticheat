# Anti-Cheat on Adversarial Clients: Engine-Level and Behavioral Defenses for Competitive PC Games

## Abstract

Client-side anti-cheat on PC runs on a machine the attacker owns. Scanning, signature matching, and kernel-level monitoring do not resolve that asymmetry. This paper examines why most commercial anti-cheat systems converge on similar techniques and share similar blind spots, then argues that the strongest long-term defenses come from engine architecture: controlling what the client knows, restricting what the client decides, and preserving enough server-side evidence for behavioral review. The focus is on publicly documented design patterns, academic research, and engineering principles that game developers can apply to make cheating structurally harder.

## Introduction

Cheating in competitive PC games is usually framed as a detection problem. Find the malicious software, flag the suspicious behavior, ban the account. The real problem sits deeper. Anti-cheat on PC operates where the attacker controls the operating system, can study any local code, and adapts tooling faster than most studios ship patches. Yan and Randell identified these dynamics in 2005, proposing a taxonomy of online game cheats based on vulnerability class, consequence, and cheating principal [1]. Collins et al. more recently placed commercial game cheats in the category of man-at-the-end (MATE) attacks, where defenders are forced to trust software running on hardware they do not control [2].

This reframes what "effective" anti-cheat can mean. Client-side defenses can raise attacker costs, shorten the lifespan of commodity cheats, and catch unsophisticated adversaries. All commercially valuable. But none of it addresses the root exposure. The better approach is to limit what the client learns, restrict what the client decides, and treat any data sent to the client as extractable. Engine architecture, replication policy, server-side validation, and behavioral analysis produce more durable gains than deeper client inspection alone [3].

The cheat economy makes this urgent. Collins et al. documented an active marketplace with substantial recurring revenue, built around subscriptions and bypass services [2]. Cheat vendors moved from basic DLL injection toward kernel components, DMA devices, and external computer-vision pipelines. Anti-cheat vendors answered with deeper inspection, higher privilege, more telemetry. Yet the defensive pattern stayed remarkably stable: inspect the client, compare state against known artifacts or heuristics, enforce. Valve's VAC documentation says outright that it "reliably detects cheats using their cheat signatures" [5]. The resemblance to antivirus is structural, not cosmetic.

The shared weaknesses follow naturally. Signature systems are reactive. Heuristic systems can be bypassed or pushed into false positives. Kernel-level drivers improve visibility but bring compatibility, privacy, and trust costs, and they do nothing about the core problem: the engine may already be shipping information to the client that should never have left the server. You cannot wallhack state that was never replicated. An aimbot loses leverage when the server keeps authority over hit outcomes [3] [6].

The bottom line is simple: as long as users can read and write to process memory on their own machine, no amount of client-side protection will stop cheating under current multiplayer engine designs. The game ships state to the client. The client holds it in addressable memory. The user (or their tools) can read that memory. Everything else in anti-cheat is a consequence of that fact.

This paper does not reverse-engineer or describe proprietary anti-cheat internals. It draws on public documentation, academic research, and observable design patterns to argue for a specific ordering of defense: minimize information leakage, minimize client authority, validate on the server, preserve evidence, then add endpoint inspection. Along the way, it covers how cheats interact with real engine internals, why players cheat, where commercial anti-cheat products fall short, and how engine architecture, memory hardening, audio-channel control, behavioral detection, community review systems, account-friction economics, and cloud gaming's structural promise and practical limits all factor into a layered defense.

## Threat Model and Scope

The scope is competitive PC multiplayer games running on adversarial client machines. The attacker controls the endpoint: the OS, user privileges, installed software, and any tools that can observe or interact with the game. Collins et al. classify this as a MATE problem [2]. Park et al. reach the same conclusion from a systems angle; once the adversary owns the machine, purely client-side defenses hit a hard ceiling [3].

Under this model, the attacker may modify game memory, intercept decrypted state, run overlays or external readers, automate input, deploy kernel-level components, or operate from a second machine entirely. Wallhacks, ESP, radar overlays, triggerbots, recoil scripts, soft and hard aimbots, and DMA-assisted reads are all in scope. DMA-based cheats deserve separate mention: a dedicated device reads game memory over PCIe from outside the OS, beyond the reach of most client-side anti-cheat.

Out of scope: match-fixing, account theft, social engineering, DDoS against infrastructure, marketplace fraud. These may overlap with cheating ecosystems but involve different engineering tradeoffs. The focus here is on cheats that alter perception, aiming, movement, or state advantage during real-time multiplayer play.

The paper assumes a standard client-server architecture: the server holds authoritative global state, clients receive updates for rendering and responsiveness, and local prediction exists for playability under latency. Park et al. use a Quake II setting to show how hidden-state exposure maps directly to wallhack utility [3]. Epic's Replication Graph documentation shows how modern engines expose explicit mechanisms for per-client replication decisions [6]. Both cases raise the same trust question: what reaches each client, when, and at what fidelity?

The defensive goal is not cheat elimination. On PC, that is unrealistic. The goal is to reduce the tactical value of exposed state, narrow client authority, raise bypass costs, preserve reviewable evidence, and keep false positives low enough to maintain player trust.

## Historical Background

Early multiplayer cheating on PC included modified executables, memory editing, packet manipulation, information leakage, timing abuse, and exploited trust assumptions baked into client-server models. Yan and Randell proposed a systematic taxonomy in 2005 that separates the *method* of cheating from the *effect* [1]. The distinction still holds. A wallhack is the result of exposing hidden game state to a client that never needed it in a tactically useful form. An aimbot exploits a tight feedback loop between visibility, target state, and input authority.

Cheats became more specialized and more commercial over time. First-person shooters created fertile ground because hidden information carries high tactical value and latency tolerance for competitive play is low. Wallhacks, triggerbots, recoil scripts, radar overlays, aim assists: they all exploit the same reality that competitive shooters must maintain substantial dynamic state locally to render, predict, and feel responsive. When too much of that state is exposed too early, a cheat converts hidden information into decisive advantage. Anti-cheat countered with injected-module scanning, tampered-memory detection, driver analysis, and known-binary signatures. Developers added replay systems, server-side sanity checks, and delayed ban waves. Collins et al. confirmed what practitioners already suspected: the market behind this is large, profitable, and professionalized [2].

Public-facing systems illustrate the constraint. Valve Anti-Cheat emphasizes signatures and delayed enforcement [5]. Epic's anti-cheat interfaces expose client/server integration points as part of a broader service stack [7]. Activision describes RICOCHET as combining a kernel-level driver that monitors applications interacting with protected titles alongside server-side analytics [8]. Each raises the cost of cheating. None removes the asymmetry inherent in running defense on a hostile endpoint.

The result is a cat-and-mouse game, and it has been one for over two decades. Anti-cheat ships an update, cheat developers reverse it, subscribers get a patched build, the cycle repeats. This is not a bug in the system; it is the system. The subscription model is what keeps the mouse funded. Collins et al. surveyed 80 cheat-selling sites and estimated combined annual revenue between $12.8 million and $73.2 million, with individual subscriptions ranging from $10 to $240 per month [2]. That money pays for full-time reverse engineers, rapid bypass turnaround, customer support, and infrastructure. When an anti-cheat update breaks a cheat, the subscription provider pushes a fix, often within days. Subscribers tolerate downtime because they are paying for maintained access, not a one-time tool. The economics are self-reinforcing: stronger anti-cheat raises cheat prices, higher prices fund better bypass engineering, and the cycle accelerates. As long as competitive games carry social and monetary stakes, this market will exist. Anti-cheat strategy needs to account for that rather than assume the adversary will eventually give up.

Players cheat for different reasons, and the reason matters for defense design. Consalvo's foundational study of cheating culture documented a wide spectrum of player motivations and introduced the concept of "gaming capital," the social currency that players accumulate through knowledge of a game's systems, including knowledge of how to exploit them [15]. Some cheat for rank or social status; Blackburn et al. analyzed the Steam Community's social graph of over 12 million gamers and found that cheaters cluster together socially, that cheating behavior correlates with social ties, and that banned players frequently return on new accounts [16]. Some act out of frustration or a sense that the system is already unfair. Some are in it for money: boosting services, account sales, tournament winnings. De Paoli and Kerr studied the commercial cheating economy in MMORPGs and showed how cheat sellers build persistent businesses around game exploits, with their own supply chains and customer relationships [17]. Others just want to see if they can get away with it. In a 2021 empirical study, Lee et al. found that competitive motivation was positively associated with cheating behavior, aggression increased cheating and was significantly related to competitive motivation, and self-esteem showed a negative relationship with cheating in their sample [4]. Anti-cheat is not just a binary technical filter. It sits inside an incentive system. The perceived probability of getting caught, the speed of enforcement, the social cost of losing an account, the structure of ranked competition: all of these shape the decision to cheat [15] [16]. Anti-cheat should be evaluated by how well it shifts attacker economics, not only by detection depth. Purely technical defenses work best alongside design choices that lower the payoff of cheating.

## How Cheats Work

Understanding why anti-cheat is hard requires knowing what cheats actually do at a technical level. Most PC game cheats fall into a few categories based on how they interact with the game process, the OS, or external hardware. Collins et al. provide a detailed technical survey of these attack methods in the context of MATE security [2].

### Memory reading and writing

The most common cheat technique is reading the game's process memory. The game engine maintains data structures for every relevant entity: player positions, health values, team IDs, bone transforms for skeletal meshes, weapon state, inventory contents. On a standard OS, any process running with sufficient privilege (or the same user context, depending on platform) can open a handle to another process and read its memory. On Windows, `ReadProcessMemory` is the textbook entry point; on Linux, `/proc/<pid>/mem` serves a similar role. A wallhack reads enemy coordinates from memory and draws them on screen. An ESP overlay does the same but renders additional metadata (health bars, distance, weapon type). The data is already there because the engine replicated it.

Call of Duty is a useful example because the IW engine descends directly from idTech 3 and the internals are well documented by the modding and reverse-engineering community. The client game module maintains a `cg_entities` array holding the current state of every entity the server has sent: position, angles, animation frame, weapon, team. A wallhack reads through that array, picks out enemy entries, projects their world coordinates onto screen space, and draws boxes or lines. An aimbot does the same read, computes the angle from the player's viewpoint to the nearest enemy head, and writes that angle into the client's `viewangles` before the next `usercmd_s` gets built. The game sends that command to the server as if the player aimed there naturally. The data is sitting in process memory, laid out in recoverable structs, and the engine has no way to tell whether a human or a cheat produced the final aim vector. This applied to CoD4 and MW2, and the same architectural pattern persists in modern titles like Warzone and MW III, though the struct layouts have evolved and obfuscation has increased.

Unreal Engine 4 and 5 follow the same pattern at larger scale. The engine's reflection system (`GObjects`, `GNames`, `FNamePool`) lets a cheat enumerate every loaded `UObject`, resolve class names at runtime, and walk the actor list from `UWorld->PersistentLevel->Actors` without hardcoded offsets. Enemy positions come from `AActor::RootComponent->RelativeLocation`. Bone-level aimbots read transforms out of `USkeletalMeshComponent` to target specific body parts. Because UE publishes its class hierarchy through reflection metadata, a cheat author does not need the game's source code to map out the relevant structures. The SDK can be reconstructed from a running process.

In both cases, the cheat logic is the same: iterate the entity list, filter for enemy players, read their world position, project it to screen coordinates, draw a box. For an aimbot, add one more step: compute the angle from the camera to the target and write it into the client's aim state (`cl.viewangles` on the IW engine, `APlayerController::ControlRotation` on Unreal). The engine packages that aim state into the next input command and sends it to the server as if the player moved the mouse there. The engine has no way to tell the difference.

Writing to memory is equally straightforward. A cheat can modify local game state to remove recoil, change movement speed, adjust viewmodel offsets, or force values that the client then reports to the server. If the server does not validate these values, the modifications take effect.

### Code injection and hooking

Rather than reading memory from the outside, many cheats inject code directly into the game process. DLL injection is the classic method: the cheat loads a shared library into the game's address space, gaining full access to its memory and function pointers. From inside the process, the cheat can hook rendering functions (to draw overlays through the game's own graphics context), intercept input handling (to modify aim before the engine processes it), or patch game logic in place.

The IW engine (Call of Duty) loads its client game logic as a separate module (`cgame_mp.dll` in CoD4-era titles). A cheat can inject alongside it and hook the syscall interface that the client game module uses to communicate with the engine core. Hooking `CG_CalcViewValues` gives direct control over the view before rendering. Hooking `CG_DrawActiveFrame` or `SCR_DrawScreenField` gives a place to draw ESP overlays using the engine's own 2D drawing functions. The modular architecture that supports modding and fast-loading zones is the same architecture that makes hooking straightforward for a cheat developer.

Unreal Engine 4/5 presents a bigger target surface. Common hook points include `UGameViewportClient::PostRender` for drawing overlays through the engine's canvas, and `ProcessEvent`, which is the virtual function that dispatches every `UFunction` call in the engine. Hooking `ProcessEvent` lets a cheat intercept any Blueprint or native function invocation, including fire events, damage application, and replication callbacks. Cheats also hook at the renderer level: patching material shaders or toggling `CustomDepthStencilValue` on enemy meshes to force them to draw through walls using the engine's own post-process pipeline.

The pattern is the same in both engines: intercept a late-stage rendering function, call the original so the game draws normally, then use the engine's own drawing API to overlay cheat visuals. The cheat renders through the same graphics context as the game, so it survives screenshots and some recording software.

Derivative engines inherit the same attack surface and the same hook points. The IW engine is one branch of a larger idTech 3 family. Quake 3, Medal of Honor: Allied Assault, Jedi Academy, Enemy Territory, Return to Castle Wolfenstein, and dozens of other titles descend from the same codebase and keep variants of `cg_entities`, `viewangles`, `usercmd_s`, the `trap_`/`R_AddCmd` rendering interface, and the syscall boundary between client game module and engine core. The struct layouts shift between forks, but a cheat developer who knows one idTech 3 game can port to another in a few hours of offset work. The same applies to Unreal-based titles. Any game on UE4 or UE5 shares `GObjects`, `GNames`, `UWorld`, `ProcessEvent`, `PostRender`, and the actor/component hierarchy. Fortnite, PUBG, Dead by Daylight, Ark, Squad, Hell Let Loose, The Finals: all present the same reflection-based attack surface with game-specific offsets. A cheat SDK generator can dump a working set of offsets from a running process in minutes. These APIs exist for legitimate engine functionality (modding, serialization, editor tooling, networking), not because anyone chose to expose them to attackers.

More broadly, common hook targets also include DirectX or Vulkan present/draw calls for rendering overlays, input pipelines for aim manipulation, and engine update functions for modifying game state each tick. Anti-cheat systems look for injected modules, suspicious memory allocations, and tampered function prologues. Cheat authors respond by using manual mapping (loading code without registering a module), erasing PE headers after load, and spoofing or proxying legitimate DLLs.

### Kernel-level cheats

When user-mode anti-cheat started scanning for injected DLLs and open process handles, cheats moved into the kernel. A kernel driver runs at ring 0 and can read or write any physical or virtual memory address without going through the normal process-handle API. This sidesteps user-mode anti-cheat entirely. The cheat driver maps the game's physical pages into its own context and reads entity data directly, or it communicates with a user-mode component through IOCTLs or shared memory.

Kernel anti-cheat (like RICOCHET or EasyAntiCheat's driver) exists partly to counter this: it monitors driver loading, checks for unsigned or suspicious kernel modules, and watches for memory access patterns that indicate another driver is reading the game process. The arms race at ring 0 is ongoing and involves driver signature forgery, vulnerable-driver exploits (bringing a legitimately signed but exploitable driver to load arbitrary code), and hypervisor-level hiding.

### DMA-based cheats

DMA (Direct Memory Access) cheats use a separate hardware device, typically a PCIe card like a modified FPGA board plugged into the target machine's motherboard. The device reads physical memory over the PCIe bus without involving the CPU or OS at all. From the perspective of the game and any anti-cheat software running on the same machine, the read never happened. There is no process handle, no system call, no driver interaction to detect.

The DMA device sends the read data to a second computer, which processes it and renders an overlay or feeds an aimbot. Some setups use HDMI capture on the second machine to combine the cheat overlay with the game's video output. This is the hardest class of cheat to detect from the client side because the cheat's execution happens entirely on different hardware. Server-side validation and restricted replication are the primary defenses; there is nothing to scan on the endpoint.

### External computer vision

CV-based cheats capture the game's rendered output (via HDMI capture card or screen capture) and run object detection models to locate enemies, healthbars, or other on-screen elements. The detection results drive an aimbot (via input injection or a hardware mouse emulator) or simply display bounding boxes on a second screen. Because the cheat never touches the game process, it is invisible to any anti-cheat that relies on process integrity, memory scanning, or driver monitoring.

The limiting factors for CV cheats are latency (capture, inference, and actuation add milliseconds) and accuracy (small or partially occluded targets are harder to detect). As inference hardware gets faster and models improve, these constraints shrink. CV-based cheats make the strongest case for restricting what the client renders in the first place: if the enemy model is not drawn because the server withheld the data, there is nothing for the vision model to detect.

### Input automation and emulation

Aimbots, triggerbots, and recoil scripts ultimately need to manipulate input. Software-based methods inject synthetic mouse or keyboard events, hook the input pipeline inside the game process, or write directly to the input device object. Hardware-based methods use microcontrollers (Arduino, Teensy, or commercial products) that sit between the real mouse/keyboard and the PC, modifying input on the wire. To the OS and anti-cheat, the input appears to come from a legitimate HID device.

Hardware input emulation is nearly undetectable from the client. The only signatures are behavioral: inhuman consistency in recoil compensation, suspicious timing between target visibility and trigger pull, or improbable correlations between aim movement and enemy position. This is why server-side behavioral analysis matters. The cheat infrastructure may be invisible, but the gameplay effects often are not, given enough data.

## Modern Anti-Cheat Pitfalls

Commercial anti-cheat systems differ in branding and deployment but converge on a common pattern: inspect the client, monitor software interacting with the game, compare observations against known bad artifacts or behavioral heuristics, enforce. Valve's VAC documentation describes signature-based cheat detection [5]. Activision describes RICOCHET as combining server-side analytics with a kernel-level driver that monitors applications interacting with protected titles [8]. Epic's anti-cheat interfaces expose a client/server integration model rather than claiming the client can be trusted alone [7].

The convergence reveals a shared limitation. These systems compensate for the fact that the attacker controls the endpoint. Collins et al. frame it explicitly as a MATE problem [2]. Once the player owns the machine, any purely client-side defense becomes a cost-imposition tool rather than a trust anchor. It can detect commodity cheats, force bypass development, generate telemetry. It cannot resolve the underlying asymmetry. The defender must protect every match at scale; the attacker needs one working bypass.

Signature-driven and artifact-driven systems are strongest against well-known public cheats and weakest against private or externally assisted tooling. Even heuristic approaches end up reactive because they are tuned against previously observed behavior. The Collins et al. marketplace analysis reinforces that this is as much an economic race as a technical one: the stronger the anti-cheat, the more valuable a durable private bypass becomes as a product [2].

Privilege escalation does not fix poor trust boundaries. A kernel-level monitor can detect a wallhack *implementation* more effectively than a user-mode scanner. It cannot undo the architectural choice that sent hidden enemy positions to an untrusted client. A kernel driver may raise the bar against local automation, but it does not replace the need for server-side hit validation, movement checks, and economy verification [3] [8].

High-privilege software also creates operational and trust costs. It widens the blast radius of bugs, complicates compatibility with overlays, capture software, accessibility tools, virtualization, and compatibility layers like Proton. Even when a vendor states its driver only runs while the protected game is active [8], the presence of kernel-level anti-cheat changes the social contract between game and player. These costs can be justified, but they should be weighed against what could have been accomplished earlier in the stack through better engine and replication design.

Client anti-cheat belongs as one layer in a larger system, not the center of gravity. The more durable gains come from reducing information leakage, reducing client authority, and shaping multiplayer state flow so that cheat tools have less material to work with. Engine-level architecture matters more than scanner depth.

## Engine-Level Defenses Against Wallhacks, Aimbots, and ESP

The key anti-cheat principle for competitive PC games: **the client should not receive sensitive state before it is needed, and it should not be trusted to decide outcomes that affect competitive integrity.** Framed this way, anti-cheat becomes less of a malware-detection problem and more of a data-exposure problem inside the game engine.

### Wallhacks as information leakage

Wallhacks are usually discussed as a rendering or overlay problem, but the root cause sits earlier in the pipeline. A wallhack becomes possible when hidden enemy state is present on the client in a recoverable form. Park et al. address this directly in BlackMirror [3]. Working with a Quake II-based prototype, they argue that client-side anti-cheat is limited by the attacker's control of the host machine, then shift defense toward *trusted visibility testing*: only visibility-confirmed data gets released for rendering. Their system performed in-enclave visibility testing, declassified only validated state, and achieved at least 97% visibility-testing accuracy with average per-frame overhead of roughly 0.57 ms.

BlackMirror matters less for its use of SGX than for the architectural lesson. If hidden entities are never exposed in tactically useful form, a wallhack loses most of its value. This generalizes past trusted execution. Most commercial games will not move rendering logic into an enclave, but they can apply the same principle: replicate enemy state based on *need to know*, not on broad world proximity. Line-of-sight checks, occlusion tests, fog-of-war rules, and coarse visibility cells reduce premature disclosure. The exact approach depends on genre. Arena shooters may need aggressive snapshot updates for responsiveness; tactical shooters can tolerate stronger filtering or coarser delayed updates for fully hidden opponents. Anti-cheat starts with deciding what each client is allowed to know at a given moment [3] [6].

### Selective replication as anti-cheat primitive

Modern engines already contain mechanisms that serve this purpose. Unreal Engine's Replication Graph is documented as a system of persistent nodes that build per-client actor replication lists on demand [6]. Epic presents it primarily as a networking and scalability feature [9], and it is, but it also gives the developer an explicit place to control which actors replicate to which clients under what conditions. A system built to reduce network overhead doubles as a tool for reducing information leakage. Replication filtering is one of the most underappreciated anti-cheat defenses available to engine programmers.

In Unreal-derived competitive games, several practical rules follow. Hidden enemies should not receive full-fidelity tactical replication unless the receiving client genuinely needs it for imminent visibility or interaction. Exact bone transforms, precise orientation, and detailed animation state do not need to reach every remote client at all times. Relevance checks should incorporate visibility logic where possible, or at minimum degrade the precision and freshness of hidden actor updates until a valid reveal condition fires. This will not make cheating impossible. It changes the problem from "extract the answer already in memory" to "infer missing state under uncertainty," which is harder and less rewarding to exploit [6] [9].

There is a performance benefit here that often goes unmentioned. Replicating fewer actors to each client means less data serialized on the server, smaller snapshots on the wire, and fewer entities deserialized and maintained in client memory. On the server side, a 100-player match that replicates every actor to every client scales at O(n^2) in update work; visibility-based filtering brings that closer to the number of actors each player can actually perceive, which is far smaller. Network bandwidth drops proportionally: withholding a hidden enemy's bone transforms, animation state, and position updates saves tens to hundreds of bytes per tick per actor per client, and that adds up fast across many players and a high tick rate. On the client side, fewer replicated entities mean fewer objects to track in memory, fewer to process through the movement and animation pipeline each frame, and less pressure on the garbage collector or memory allocator. For large-scale modes (battle royale, big-team playlists, MMO-adjacent designs), tighter replication filtering can be the difference between hitting frame budgets and missing them. The security argument and the performance argument point in the same direction: send less.

### Snapshot architecture lessons still apply

The snapshot model that defined early id Software engines has not gone away. BlackMirror used Quake II because its snapshot architecture and local prediction make it easy to trace where hidden state becomes exposed [3]. The standard pattern persists in modern titles: the server maintains global game state, periodically sends updates to each client, the client maintains local state for rendering and prediction. Call of Duty, Battlefield, Overwatch, Valorant, and every Unreal-derived shooter still work this way at the networking level. The anti-cheat takeaway is that every server-to-client update is a *declassification decision*, not just a networking event. Whenever the server sends enemy position, angle, or movement state to a client that cannot yet legitimately perceive it, the engine is precomputing cheat value for the adversary.

Practical defenses in snapshot-based engines include tighter PVS logic, stricter culling of fully occluded opponents, coarser update granularity for actors behind geometry, and server-side delay of sensitive state until a reveal threshold is crossed. These choices cost something. Over-filtering can cause pop-in, exacerbate peeker's advantage, or create discrepancies between what a player saw and what the server believed was visible. These are engineering tradeoffs, not arguments against the principle. A wallhack thrives on generosity with hidden information.

### Aimbot resistance through server authority

Aimbots present a different problem because they exploit both hidden information and the fast local aiming loop that shooters demand. Engine design still matters. A client should not be the final authority on whether a shot hit, whether recoil was followed, whether aim snapped through a physically impossible arc, or whether input sequences were plausible over time. Server-authoritative hit validation, movement sanity checks, and replayable event logs do not eliminate aimbots, but they force the cheat to operate within tighter physical and temporal constraints. Bethea et al. demonstrated that server-side verification of client behavior can catch executions inconsistent with sanctioned play [13].

Aimbot detection also benefits from structured telemetry. Research on passive cheat detection has shown that aimbots leave measurable traces in gameplay data even when they run in a semi-automated, "human-interactive" mode [10]. Reaction time distributions, angular acceleration profiles, target-switch geometry, recoil compensation consistency, prefire timing: all carry signal. Not every suspicious flick is cheating, and statistical models should not serve as judge and jury. But engines should produce the telemetry needed for behavioral validation rather than relying entirely on runtime process inspection [10] [13].

### Replay, auditability, and multi-match context

A common anti-cheat mistake is trying to decide everything in real time from a narrow observation window. Engine design can help by creating better evidence pipelines. Server-side replays, shot timelines, input event summaries, and actor-state histories let studios evaluate suspicious behavior in context rather than from a single local snapshot. This is most important for soft aimbots and low-FOV aim assistance, which are specifically designed to blend into human play. The engine should preserve enough authoritative history to support review across multiple matches. A cheat may look plausible in one highlight clip but statistically implausible across hundreds of engagements [10].

### ESP and radar as symptoms of over-replication

ESP and radar cheats are usually discussed as memory-reading problems, but the deeper cause is architectural. If hidden enemies, objectives, or loot states are replicated too early or too precisely, an external overlay is just converting already-available local state into a friendlier display. Encrypting packets does not help once the data has been decrypted and placed in client memory. Reducing the amount, precision, and timing of sensitive state delivered before it becomes tactically justified is a stronger defense than anti-overlay tricks. Visibility-aware replication, interest management, and delayed disclosure address the root cause [3] [6].

### Audio as an information channel

The information-leakage argument in this paper focuses on entity positions, bone transforms, and animation state. Spatial audio is another disclosure vector. If the engine sends full 3D positional audio data for all nearby entities regardless of visual occlusion, a cheat can derive approximate enemy positions from audio parameters alone, even after selective replication has withheld visual state. Sound occlusion filtering, attenuating or withholding audio events based on wall geometry, distance, and material properties, is the audio analog of visibility-based replication. Some engines already do basic audio occlusion for immersion, but few treat it as a security boundary with the same rigor applied to entity replication.

The limitation is practical. Accurate audio occlusion is computationally expensive, especially in complex geometry with destructible environments and dynamic openings. Aggressive sound filtering risks making the game feel acoustically dead, and players depend on audio cues for legitimate tactical play: hearing footsteps through a thin wall, catching a reload around a corner. The fidelity tradeoff is sharper than in visual replication. Withholding a hidden enemy's position data costs the legitimate player nothing. Muting or degrading sounds that *should* be faintly audible through a wall degrades everyone's experience. In practice, most engines apply coarse distance and obstruction checks rather than full propagation simulation, which still leaks enough positional data for a purpose-built reader to extract value. Audio-based information control remains a real gap even if practical solutions are harder to deploy cleanly than visual replication filtering.

### Memory-layout hardening

This paper argues for engine-level defense but focuses primarily on replication filtering and server authority. There is another class of engine-level defense that operates on the client itself: making local memory harder to interpret. Pointer encryption, struct-layout randomization, address-space re-randomization per session, and decoy data structures all increase the cost of reading or writing game state from process memory. Activision has described elements of this approach in the context of RICOCHET [8], and cheat forums routinely document the additional reverse-engineering effort required when a title obfuscates its internal structures on a per-update basis.

These techniques genuinely raise attacker costs, especially for commodity cheats that depend on fixed offsets or publicly shared SDKs. When the memory layout shifts with every patch, or when pointer chains are encrypted, offset tables break and need rebuilding. This slows the cheat development pipeline and shortens the usable window between an anti-cheat update and a working bypass.

The limitation is that obfuscation is delay, not prevention. A motivated reverse engineer with a debugger and enough time will recover the layout. Runtime decryption and de-randomization tools can be built into cheat loaders. The engine itself must still read its own data, so the decryption keys and layout mappings exist somewhere in the process and can be found. Obfuscation also adds runtime overhead: encrypted pointer dereferences, randomized struct access, and integrity checks cost CPU cycles on every frame. In latency-sensitive competitive games, that cost is not free. Memory hardening works best as a friction layer that complements replication filtering and server authority, not as a standalone defense. It makes life harder for cheat authors without addressing the core problem that the data exists on the client at all.

## Machine Learning, Behavioral Detection, and Trust Scoring

Machine learning is frequently pitched as the next major answer to cheating. The framing is too broad and usually too optimistic. In practice, ML works best as a server-side evidence amplifier. It helps identify behavioral patterns that are hard to catch through client inspection alone, especially when cheats minimize their local footprint or avoid signature-based detection. Its effectiveness depends on strong engine telemetry, server-authoritative outcomes, and a review pipeline that can evaluate play across time [10] [11].

### ML as behavioral layer, not trust anchor

The biggest conceptual mistake is treating ML as a replacement for the harder engineering work. An engine that over-replicates hidden state, trusts the client for hit registration, or fails to record enough data for retrospective analysis is giving ML weak evidence to work with. When the engine already enforces server authority and records structured telemetry about aim behavior, movement, visibility, and timing, ML becomes a viable triage and prioritization system. When the engine produces good anti-cheat data, behavioral detection actually works [10] [13].

External and low-footprint cheats make this relevant. A modern cheat does not need obvious code injection. It may read state from a second machine, apply computer vision to the screen, or operate as a "soft" assist tool that leaves the player partially in control. Client inspection alone may miss these entirely. Behavioral analysis looks at outcomes visible from the server side rather than relying on process-level artifacts [10] [12].

### Aimbots leave statistical traces

The most promising ML application in shooter anti-cheat is aimbot detection. Liu et al. proposed detecting passive cheats through *performance-skillfulness inconsistency*, using gameplay and input-derived features rather than invasive endpoint inspection [10]. The relevant features are reaction-time distributions, angular aim deltas, target-switch behavior, recoil compensation regularity, crosshair settling patterns, and the relationship between coarse input skill and precise combat outcomes. A soft aimbot may avoid impossible flicks, but over many engagements it tends to compress variance in ways human play does not. Unnaturally consistent micro-corrections, stable target acquisition under stress, or a gap between movement skill and combat precision are all detectable with enough data [10] [11].

The literature also warns against overclaiming. Witschel and Wressnegger showed in their EuroSec paper that aimbot detectors can be evaded by cheats that deliberately mimic human-like movement [12]. ML does not end the arms race. It shifts part of the race from code signatures into feature design, adversarial robustness, and longitudinal analysis.

### Multi-match context beats single-frame judgment

Cheating usually becomes more obvious in aggregate. A single suspicious flick or prefire may be inconclusive. Across dozens of matches, repeated low-latency target acquisition, unusually stable recoil correction, improbable success under partial visibility, or persistent gaps between movement quality and combat precision can become statistically meaningful. Behavioral models improve when they draw on sessions, maps, weapon classes, and opponent populations [10].

A game that wants ML-assisted anti-cheat should preserve enough authoritative data to reconstruct combat behavior over time: server-side shot timelines, aim deltas, hit outcomes, visibility states, movement context, engagement ranges, input summaries. Without those features, ML degrades to inference from coarse scoreboard stats or compressed event logs. With them, it becomes a useful ranking and prioritization tool [10] [13].

### Trust scoring over binary classification

One of the most practical ML uses in deployment is *trust scoring* rather than auto-banning. A model estimates the likelihood that recent behavior is inconsistent with the player's historical profile or with the expected distribution of legitimate play. That score gets combined with other evidence: client integrity signals, ban-evasion indicators, report volume, hardware churn, account age, matchmaking anomalies, replay review. Layering is safer than using a classifier as a single source of truth, especially in competitive games where false positives carry real cost [11] [13].

Epic's anti-cheat documentation references "training and application of machine-learning models for detection of aim-assisting cheats in shooter games," based on server data about player actions [7] [14]. Even major platforms present ML as one layer in a broader system, not a standalone solution.

### Limitations

ML models are only as good as their training data. If training over-represents obvious public cheats and under-represents subtle assists, production performance will disappoint. Player skill is heterogeneous; legitimately elite players can produce aim traces and reaction times that look anomalous relative to the median. Adversarial adaptation is unavoidable. Once cheat authors learn which behaviors are risky, they randomize aim paths, inject delay, widen assist cones, or tune automation to stay below thresholds. Witschel and Wressnegger demonstrate this concretely [12].

There is also a governance problem. A system that cannot explain why a player was flagged is hard to defend internally or externally. The better operational pattern is to use ML to surface suspicious accounts, then ground the final decision in interpretable evidence: replay segments, statistically unusual aim distributions, visibility anomalies, repeated trust-score escalation, or corroborating platform telemetry. The model allocates attention. It should not be an unquestioned oracle [11] [13].

### Practical ML recommendations

1. Feed ML primarily with server-authoritative and audit-friendly telemetry: state the server already trusts or can independently verify. Models trained on data the attacker can trivially manipulate are less useful than they appear.
2. Design for ranking, triage, and trust scoring, not hard binary classification. This makes it easier to incorporate uncertainty, adapt thresholds by playlist or skill bracket, and limit false-positive damage.
3. Collect features over long enough windows to capture stable patterns. The strongest signals emerge across many engagements, not from one clip.
4. Pair ML output with replay and analyst workflows. A high-confidence flag should be explainable through evidence a human investigator can inspect.
5. Treat ML as a complement to engine hardening, never a substitute. If the engine still leaks hidden state or over-trusts the client, ML is compensating for an earlier architectural mistake.

### Community review and human-in-the-loop enforcement

The paper recommends replay and auditability but stops short of the operational implementation that closes the loop: community-driven review systems. CS:GO's Overwatch system (now inherited by CS2), Riot's reporting-to-review pipeline, and similar programs put curated replay evidence in front of volunteer or contracted human reviewers. These programs connect the audit-quality telemetry discussed above to actual enforcement decisions on ambiguous cases.

Community review has real strengths. Human reviewers can contextualize behavior that statistical models struggle with: unconventional playstyles, unusually skilled legitimate players, gamesense-driven prefires that look suspicious in a highlight clip but make sense in full match context. A well-designed review program also distributes enforcement labor across the player base and creates a social norm around fair play. When players see that reports lead to review and review leads to consequences, trust in the system increases.

The limitations are substantial. Volunteer reviewers are inconsistent. Inter-rater reliability varies, and reviewers bring their own biases: rank-based assumptions, character or weapon prejudice, different personal thresholds for what counts as suspicious. Subtle cheats, exactly the soft aimbots and low-FOV assists that this paper highlights as hardest to catch, are also hardest for human reviewers to identify from a replay. Review throughput is finite. Popular games generate more reports than any review system can process in real time, which means most cases still need automated triage before a human sees them. Community review can also be gamed through coordinated false reports, vote-brigading, or deliberate sandbagging to stay below reviewer attention. Community review works best as a middle tier: ML and automated scoring handle volume and surface the ambiguous cases, human reviewers handle the gray zone, and the game records enough evidence to support both [10] [13].

## Design Recommendations

The prior sections converge on a practical ordering of defensive priorities for competitive PC games:

1. **Minimize what the client learns.** Treat replication as a security boundary. Review replication code with the same rigor as authentication logic. Hidden enemies, objectives, and tactically decisive metadata should be withheld, coarsened, delayed, or revealed only when the game rules require it [3] [6].

2. **Minimize what the client decides.** Clients may predict movement and present local effects for responsiveness, but consequential outcomes (damage, hit registration, movement legality, economy transitions, inventory changes, rank effects) should stay under server authority [13].

3. **Validate consequential actions on the server.** Server-side verification narrows the surface on which cheats can directly force state changes. It also produces better evidence for later review because the server becomes the authoritative record of what happened [13].

4. **Preserve evidence for retrospective analysis.** Build the engine to support behavioral review, not only real-time intervention. Record enough structured history to reconstruct aim behavior, visibility conditions, damage timing, and movement plausibility over time. Without this evidence layer, studios are stuck choosing between weak real-time heuristics and invasive endpoint inspection [10] [13].

5. **Use ML for ranking and triage.** Behavioral models work best as a trust-scoring layer built on server-authoritative evidence, not as infallible classifiers. Pair them with replay review and corroborating signals [10] [12].

6. **Use client anti-cheat to support the architecture, not replace it.** Anti-cheat products provide real value against commodity cheats and generate useful telemetry. But when the engine already leaks hidden state or over-trusts the client, the scanner is fighting uphill. Client anti-tamper belongs as the outer layer, not the foundation [2] [5] [8].

7. **Optimize for attacker cost and false-positive restraint.** No architecture eliminates cheating on PC. The goal is to make bypass expensive, shorten cheat lifetimes, reduce the tactical value of extracted information, and keep enforcement accurate enough to maintain player trust.

8. **Design for privacy and regulatory compliance from the start.** Any anti-cheat system that collects telemetry, behavioral profiles, hardware fingerprints, or process inventories needs to account for GDPR, regional data-protection laws, and platform-specific requirements. Kernel-level anti-cheat is particularly sensitive; deeper inspection means a wider data-collection surface, and "catching cheaters" does not automatically satisfy data-minimization requirements. Studios should involve legal counsel early, disclose data practices clearly, retain data only as long as needed, and build opt-out or consent mechanisms where required.

9. **Raise the cost of re-entry after a ban.** Anti-cheat enforcement loses its teeth when a banned player can return on a new account within minutes at no cost. Phone-number verification (CS2 Prime, Valorant's SMS requirement), hardware-identifier bans, account-age gating for ranked queues, and paid-game economics all increase the friction of ban evasion. The stronger the re-entry barrier, the higher the effective cost of getting caught, and the more weight a ban carries as a deterrent. The limitation is that every friction mechanism aimed at cheaters also affects legitimate players. HWID bans can hit shared machines: internet cafes, family PCs, secondhand hardware with a previous owner's ban. Phone verification excludes players without qualifying phone numbers and raises privacy concerns in regions with strict SIM-registration laws. Paid-game requirements penalize legitimate new players and create marketplace incentives for bulk account farming. HWID spoofing tools exist and are actively maintained, so hardware bans are a cost increase, not a hard block. Re-entry friction is a useful lever but an imperfect one; it works best alongside the behavioral detection and replication controls described above, not as an independent solution.

This ordering matters. Each layer becomes more effective when the layers below it have already reduced the attacker's leverage. A game cannot reliably scan its way out of over-replication and over-trust.

## Conclusion

Anti-cheat on PC is a trust-boundary problem on an untrusted endpoint. Commercial anti-cheat products do useful work: they detect commodity cheats, raise engineering costs for cheat authors, shorten bypass lifetimes, generate enforcement telemetry. They do not change the central asymmetry. The attacker owns the machine.

The strongest improvements come from places not traditionally marketed as "anti-cheat." Replication policy, visibility-aware state disclosure, server-authoritative outcomes, replayability, audit-quality telemetry, multi-match behavioral review. All of these strip cheat value before any scanner runs. You cannot wallhack information the server never sent. An aimbot loses teeth when the server constrains hit outcomes and keeps enough evidence to evaluate suspicious behavior over time. Park et al. demonstrated this for wallhacks [3]. Epic's documentation shows that modern engines already provide hooks for selective replication and ML-assisted detection built on server data [6] [7] [14].

The practical recommendation is not to abandon client anti-cheat but to demote it from foundation to outer layer. The Design Recommendations section lays out the specific ordering. Bethea et al.'s server-verification work [13] and Liu et al.'s passive-detection research [10] both support this layered view.

ML fits into this architecture as a behavioral triage and trust-scoring mechanism built on server-authoritative data. Not as an infallible classifier. Not as a substitute for engine hardening. Witschel and Wressnegger showed that behavioral models become part of an ongoing arms race once deployed [12]. That does not make ML useless. It means it should be deployed with restraint, explainability, and corroborating evidence.

No anti-cheat architecture on PC will eliminate cheating. The realistic goal is to make cheating more expensive, less reliable, less scalable, and less rewarding, while keeping the game fair for legitimate competitors and avoiding enforcement methods that erode trust more than they protect it. Effective anti-cheat is not a single product, driver, or model. It is disciplined engineering for running a competitive game where the client cannot be fully trusted.

The logical endpoint of the MATE argument is removing the adversarial client entirely. Cloud gaming services (GeForce Now, Xbox Cloud Gaming, Amazon Luna) run the game on provider-controlled infrastructure and stream video to the player. The client never receives game state in memory. There is no process to read, no network traffic carrying entity positions, no rendering pipeline to hook. Under this model, the only remaining cheat surface is input automation and external computer vision applied to the video stream, both constrained by capture latency, and both addressable through server-side behavioral checks.

Cloud gaming is not a practical universal answer today. Input latency remains a competitive disadvantage for fast-paced shooters, which is the genre where cheating pressure is highest. Players who care most about competitive integrity also care most about responsiveness, and streaming introduces unavoidable round-trip delay that native hardware does not. Infrastructure costs limit geographic availability. Many players prefer local hardware for performance tuning, peripheral customization, and offline access. Cloud platforms themselves become high-value attack targets: if compromised at the provider level, they could expose many players simultaneously rather than one at a time. And the provider must still trust its own server infrastructure against insider threats and supply-chain attacks. Cloud gaming eliminates the MATE problem in its classical form, but the practical tradeoffs keep it from replacing native PC play for competitive titles in the near term. It is worth watching as infrastructure matures, not worth treating as a solution that is about to arrive.

## Limitations of This Paper

This paper has several deliberate constraints worth stating.

First, it does not describe how commercial anti-cheat products work internally. Anti-cheat vendors protect their detection methods through trade secrets, license agreements, and legal enforcement under the DMCA and similar statutes. Reverse-engineering or publicly detailing active detection techniques risks both legal liability and directly helping cheat developers bypass those systems. For that reason, all discussion here is limited to publicly documented interfaces, published academic research, and general architectural principles. Where a vendor's documentation describes a capability (e.g., Valve's signature-based approach [5], Activision's kernel driver and server analytics [8]), those descriptions are cited at face value without speculation about undisclosed internals.

Second, the paper focuses on PC competitive shooters. The arguments about client trust, replication policy, and behavioral detection apply broadly, but the specifics (kernel cheats, DMA devices, ring-0 arms races) are platform-dependent. Console and mobile environments have different trust models and different attack surfaces.

Third, the referenced studies have their own scope limits. Park et al. prototyped BlackMirror on a Quake II engine, not a production title [3]. Liu et al. evaluated passive detection on a specific dataset [10]. Collins et al. surveyed the cheat marketplace at a single point in time [2]. Revenue figures, detection rates, and bypass timelines will shift as the market evolves.

Fourth, the paper argues for a specific defensive ordering but does not provide implementation benchmarks. The performance claims about selective replication (reduced bandwidth, lower server CPU, smaller client memory footprint) are directionally supported by Epic's documentation [6] [9] but have not been independently measured here against a production title at scale.

Finally, no anti-cheat paper written from the public side of the fence can be complete. The most operationally relevant information, what specific detections work, what bypass techniques are in active use, what telemetry signals are strongest, sits behind NDAs and active enforcement programs. This paper works with what is openly available and tries to be honest about where that leaves gaps.
